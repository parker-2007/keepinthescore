From Recruiting to Re-Signing; College Football's NIL Problem.

Written by Ryan Sconzo • Opinion • College Football

For years, college football sold itself on one simple promise: this wasn’t the NFL. It was regional, emotional, and rooted in school pride rather than contracts and cap space. That line has now been completely blurred, and one recent headline shows just how far the sport has drifted.

“South Carolina quarterback LaNorris Sellers is nearing a deal to return to the school for 2026, sources told ESPN’s Pete Thamel.”

Read that again. A deal to return for a future season. That is not a sentence that should exist in college football. This isn’t free agency. This isn’t contract extension season. Or at least, it wasn’t supposed to be.

Name, Image, and Likeness (NIL) was introduced with good intentions. To allow athletes to profit from their brand fairly. College sports have now become an unregulated market. Adding NIL to the deal for athletes makes college sports look a lot like professional sports, causing college-sport fans to lose their spirit. LaNorra Sellers did nothing wrong here. The system incentivizes players to protect their value, and any athlete would be foolish not to take advantage of leverage in today’s game. The problem isn’t the player; it’s that college football now speaks the language of contracts and negotiations.

College teams used to be built through recruiting, development, and patience. Players stayed, grew into roles, and became faces of programs. Now, rosters flip year to year. As of January 16, 3,125 D1 athletes have entered this year's transfer portal. Continuity, once a defining trait of college football, is disappearing.

The Sellers headline also highlights another uncomfortable truth: NIL has quietly legalized pay-for-play without any restrictions. Many deals have little to do with actual endorsements and everything to do with keeping talent on campus. Collectives function like payroll departments, and booster money dictates competitive balance more than coaching or culture.

Fans feel it too. It’s harder to get attached to players when they might be gone next season, or when their return feels like a transaction instead of loyalty. Jersey sales, traditions, rivalries, and long-term storylines all suffer when college football becomes a short-term business.

And for smaller programs, NIL has turned them into farm systems. Develop a player, watch him break out, then watch a bigger school outbid you the next offseason. The gap between the powerhouses and small programs continues to get larger.

Again, this is not an argument against paying players. They deserve compensation. But college football was never meant to operate like the pros, and pretending otherwise is dishonest. When headlines read like NFL free agency updates, something has gone wrong.

If college football wants to survive as its own product, not just a cheaper, messier NFL, NIL needs structure, transparency, and limits. Otherwise, the sport risks losing the very identity that lured fans into fandom in the first place.
