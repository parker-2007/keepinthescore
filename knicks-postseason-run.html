Records Were Made to Be Broken: The 2026 NBA Playoffs Are Unlike Anything We've Ever Seen

Written by Ryan Sconzo • Analysis • NBA Playoffs

The NBA postseason has always been the stage where legends are made and history is written. But the 2026 playoffs aren't just writing history — they're shredding the record books entirely. Through the first two rounds, this postseason has produced a staggering number of all-time marks, from a team that is literally the most dominant playoff unit ever assembled on a points basis to individual performances that have never been seen in the history of the sport. Buckle up.

The New York Knicks: The Most Dominant Two-Round Run Ever

Let's start with the headliner, because there is no other way to frame what the New York Knicks have done through ten playoff games: they have been the most dominant team in NBA postseason history by point differential.

The Knicks have outscored their opponents by 194 points across their first ten playoff games — the best point differential in a 10-game span in NBA playoff history. For context, the previous record of +170 was set by the 2017 Golden State Warriors, a team widely regarded as one of the greatest ever assembled. New York didn't just edge that mark. They crushed it.

Over those ten games, the Knicks have outscored opponents by 19.4 points per game — the best per-game differential for any team through two rounds in the 43 years of the 16-team playoff format. They've won seven consecutive postseason games to close out the run, a new franchise record for consecutive wins in a single postseason.

The Halftime from Another Dimension vs. Atlanta

Before they turned the Sixers into rubble, the Knicks served notice in the first round against the Atlanta Hawks. In their series-clinching blowout, the Knicks led by as many as 61 points and won by a 51-point margin — the largest playoff victory in franchise history.

Their 47-point halftime advantage broke a record previously held by both the 2017 Cleveland Cavaliers and the 2025 Indiana Pacers. The Hawks scored just 36 points in the first half and committed 14 turnovers as the Knicks turned what should have been a competitive playoff game into a national event.

The Three-Point Quarter That Rewrote the Record Book vs. Philadelphia

If the Atlanta game was the appetizer, Game 4 against Philadelphia was the main course — and the Knicks served it flaming hot from beyond the arc.

In the first quarter alone, the Knicks hit 11-of-13 from three-point range, tying the NBA playoff record for the most three-pointers made in a single quarter. Miles McBride, who started in place of the injured OG Anunoby, became the first Knick since play-by-play tracking began in 1997 to hit four three-pointers in the first quarter of a playoff game, draining them on four consecutive possessions to turn a modest lead into a rout before the first break.

By the final buzzer, New York had tied the NBA record for the most team three-pointers made in a playoff game with 25, joining the 2021 Milwaukee Bucks and the 2016 Cleveland Cavaliers as the only franchises to reach that mark. McBride led the barrage with seven three-pointers, finishing with 25 points. Jalen Brunson added six, Josh Hart four, and Landry Shamet four more as the Knicks turned Wells Fargo Center into a home game.

New York's three-point efficiency was staggering — having shot 19-for-37 (51%) from deep in Game 1, the Knicks became just the third team and the first in five years to shoot better than 50% on at least 35 three-point attempts twice in the same playoff series.

The offensive efficiency of the game was equally absurd. Through 36 minutes, New York had scored 122 points on 71 possessions at a rate of 172 points per 100 — the most efficient game for any team in the 30 seasons for which play-by-play data exists. Garbage time pulled the number back slightly, but the Knicks still won by 30, their seventh double-digit victory of the postseason.

As for Joel Embiid, he went out with his head held high — posting 24 points on remarkably efficient shooting in a closeout game — doing everything he could to keep his team alive. It wasn't enough. The Sixers have still not advanced past the second round since 2001.

Donovan Mitchell Ties a Legend

While the Knicks were making history in the East, Donovan Mitchell was carving his own name into the record books in Cleveland's Game 4 showdown with the Detroit Pistons.

Mitchell scored 43 points in total, with a mind-bending 39 of them coming in the second half alone — tying Eric "Sleepy" Floyd for the most points scored in a single half in NBA playoff history. The Cavaliers also put together a franchise playoff-record 24–0 run in the game, one of the most stunning momentum swings of the entire postseason. The series is now tied 2–2, and Mitchell has announced himself as one of the most dangerous players remaining.

Out West: OKC Is Perfect, and History Is Everywhere

The Eastern Conference doesn't have a monopoly on the record-breaking. The Western Conference has been equally wild.

The Oklahoma City Thunder completed a sweep of the Los Angeles Lakers to advance to the Western Conference Finals with a perfect 8–0 playoff record. OKC's eight consecutive wins against the Lakers this season mark the longest winning streak against Los Angeles in a single season since the 1976–77 post-merger era. Shai Gilgeous-Alexander delivered another signature closing performance, finishing with 35 points and eight assists while shooting 11-of-22 from the field to seal the series.

The Thunder have also won the Maurice Podoloff Trophy — awarded to the team with the best overall record — for the second straight year, becoming the first franchise to accomplish that feat.

Meanwhile, in the Spurs–Timberwolves series, Victor Wembanyama has been nothing short of supernatural when healthy. In Game 1 of the Western Conference Semifinals, Wembanyama set a new NBA single-game playoff record with 12 blocked shots, surpassing the previous mark of 10 held by Mark Eaton, Hakeem Olajuwon, and Andrew Bynum. He added 15 rebounds and 11 points in a defensive performance unlike anything previously seen on a playoff stage.

Even the Cavaliers–Raptors first-round series produced history. In Game 3, LeBron James lobbed an alley-oop to his son Bronny James for a reverse layup — the first father-son assist in NBA playoff history.

What Does It All Mean?

It is genuinely rare for a single postseason to produce this many record-setting moments. Usually, one team does something historic. One player has a legendary series. One game gets circled on the calendar forever. The 2026 playoffs are doing all of it simultaneously across every corner of the bracket.

The Knicks are the story of the moment — a team playing at a level that no franchise has ever sustained through two rounds of the playoffs. Donovan Mitchell is reminding everyone he's built for this stage. Victor Wembanyama is rewriting what we thought was physically possible on the defensive end. The Thunder are marching toward a potential dynasty. And we're not even in the Conference Finals yet.

The 2026 playoffs aren't just historic. They may be the most statistically remarkable postseason the NBA has ever seen.
